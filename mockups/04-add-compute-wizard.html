Spend at most $ and run at most hours.

  • Nothing provisions until first use — registration itself is free.
  • A launch that couldn't finish under the cap is refused up front (budget.exceeded).
  • A watchdog checks on every poll: past the cap it cancels jobs, shuts the instance down, then errors.
  • Tear down on the site page is the off switch, any time.

Design notes

  1. Saved hosts first. Your existing saved connections become the primary picker — name, address, and readiness at a glance. Typing an address by hand is the fallback, not the default.
  2. Reuse what works. weft connects the same way your own tools already do, so every convention you rely on carries over untouched — the wizard says this instead of hoping you assume it.
  3. Failure is a first-class path. A failed connection becomes a plain-language reason plus targeted fixes, most likely first — never a bare "connection failed". The full message from the cluster is always available verbatim.
  4. Setup happens on your machine. Any step that needs your sign-in runs in a terminal on your own computer; weft only waits for it to finish and never handles your sign-in itself.
  5. The agent is the escape hatch. Every stuck state offers "ask the agent", opening chat pre-seeded with the wizard context. Same flow, two frontends — the peer principle.
  6. Guided storage choice. The disk probe ranks candidates and the copy explains the reasoning (rebuildable state → scratch; purges survivable; precious data → the large-files role) rather than just flagging.
  7. Honest discovery. Account comes from the scheduler when visible; when it isn't, the field says "not visible" and stays optional — no invented defaults.
  8. Policy as sentences. Controls sit inside plain language, and the enforced (structured rules, checked at submit) vs surfaced (free-text notes, shown in every plan) distinction is stated exactly — it builds the right trust.
  9. Probe narration. Stage-by-stage progress (today wrapped UI-side; a bootstrap.step event is a small upstream ask). The compute-node check rides a 1-core job through the shortest partition.
  10. Facts carry consequences. "No compute internet" is immediately translated into what weft does about it (packed environments) — this is the "scoping out remote compute" deliverable in one sheet. A musl-libc result would render the same way: "conda envs impossible here; bare tasks OK".
  11. Money can't be skipped. The cloud budget block is required, and the watchdog semantics (refuse up front / cancel + shut down + error on breach / manual off switch) are spelled out where the numbers are set.
